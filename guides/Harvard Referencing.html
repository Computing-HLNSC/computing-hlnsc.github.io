Referencing, Citations & Reference Lists

Referencing is the process of acknowledging the sources you have used in your academic work. It shows where your information came from, supports your arguments with evidence, and demonstrates honesty and academic integrity.

Why we reference:
Referencing prevents plagiarism, gives credit to original authors, strengthens your writing with research, and allows tutors and readers to verify your sources.

What referencing is:
It is a system used to provide full details of every source you have used. In academic work, the Harvard referencing system is commonly used. A reference entry includes information such as the author, year, title, publisher, and URL (if online).

What citations are:
Citations are brief notes placed in the main body of your writing. They link directly to your reference list and are used every time you quote, paraphrase, summarise, or use someone else‚Äôs ideas. A Harvard citation usually contains the author‚Äôs surname and the year, for example: (Smith, 2023).

What a reference list is:
The reference list is found at the end of your assignment. It contains the full details of every source you cited in your work, organised alphabetically by the author's surname. It must only include sources you have actually cited.

Plagiarism

Plagiarism is presenting someone else‚Äôs words, ideas, or work as your own without proper referencing. This includes copying, paraphrasing without acknowledgement, or using material from AI tools, websites, books, or other students without citing it. Referencing correctly protects you from plagiarism and shows academic honesty.
